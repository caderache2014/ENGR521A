------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT THIS IS
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Julia implementation of the RomeroNNV hybrid neural ODE from:

  Wang, Garnier & Rea. "Hybridizing physics and neural ODEs for
  predicting plasma inductance dynamics in tokamak fusion reactors."
  arXiv:2310.20079v1, 2023.

The model replaces the approximate third equation in Romero (2010)'s
3-state lumped-parameter ODE with a neural network residual correction,
while keeping the two exact physics equations intact. The three states
are Li (internal inductance), Ip (plasma current), and V (loop voltage
at the equilibrium flux surface). Vind (inductive voltage from the
external coils) is a control input, not a state — it is provided as a
cubic spline interpolant built from each shot's data.

IMPLEMENTATION
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Language: Julia 1.x
Key packages:
  DifferentialEquations.jl / OrdinaryDiffEq.jl  — ODE solves
  Lux.jl                                         — neural network
  DiffEqFlux.jl                                  — multiple shooting
  SciMLSensitivity.jl (InterpolatingAdjoint)     — adjoint gradients
  Optimization.jl + OptimizationOptimisers.jl    — AdamW optimizer
  Dierckx.jl                                     — Vind splines
  ComponentArrays.jl / Flux (destructure only)   — parameter handling

Neural network architecture (RomeroNNV):
  Input:  [Li, Ip, V, Vind]  (4 features)
  Hidden: Dense(4→32, softplus) → Dense(32→32, softplus)
  Output: Dense(32→1, tanh)   — bounded residual correction to dV/dt
  Parameters: 1249 total

ODE right-hand side:
  du[1] = (-2*Vind - 2*V) / (liip_norm * |Ip|)   # exact Romero
  du[2] = ( 2*Vind +   V) / (liip_norm * |Li|)   # exact Romero
  du[3] = -V/τ + (κ/τ)*Vind + NN(Li,Ip,V,Vind)  # Romero + NN residual

Physics constants: κ=0.98, τ=1.25 (from Romero 2010)
Normalization: liip_norm = max(|Ip|) × max(|Li|) across all 489 shots
               = 3.6559 (MA × dimensionless)

WHY WE BUILD A SPLINE FROM Vind — CONTROLS VS STATES
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The LIV system has two types of variables that play fundamentally
different roles in the ODE.

STATES are the variables the ODE integrates forward in time:
  Li, Ip, and V. The solver starts from initial conditions at t=0
  and advances the state vector step by step. At each step it calls
  the right-hand side function to compute dLi/dt, dIp/dt, and dV/dt,
  then uses those derivatives to update the states. The neural network
  learns to correct dV/dt. States are what we predict and compare
  against measured data in the loss function.

CONTROLS are external inputs that drive the system but are not
  predicted by the model. Vind (the inductive voltage from the
  external poloidal field coils) is a control — it is set by the
  tokamak operator and measured directly. The ODE needs to evaluate
  Vind at every internal timestep the solver takes, including steps
  between the measured data points.

THE PROBLEM: The ODE solver (Tsit5) is adaptive — it takes timesteps
  of varying size determined by the error tolerance, not by the
  spacing of the experimental data. Our Vind measurements exist only
  at ~28-46 discrete times per shot. The solver may need to evaluate
  Vind hundreds of times per shot at arbitrary intermediate times.

THE SOLUTION: We fit a cubic spline to the measured (time, Vind)
  pairs for each shot before training begins. The spline is a smooth
  C2-continuous function t → Vind(t) that passes through all measured
  points and can be evaluated at any time in the shot duration. When
  the ODE solver queries Vind at an arbitrary t, it calls the spline
  instead of interpolating the raw data.

  spl_s = Spline1D(t_shot, Vind_shot; k=3, bc="extrapolate")
  Vind(t) = spl_s(t)   # callable at any t

WHY NOT SPLINE Li, Ip, OR V? If we splined the states and fed them
  into the ODE right-hand side, we would be giving the solver the
  measured answer at each step. The ODE would have no reason to learn
  anything — it would just read out the ground truth. This is called
  "teacher forcing" and it prevents the model from learning real
  dynamics. States must be integrated freely from initial conditions
  so that training errors propagate back through the ODE and teach
  the network to correct its behavior.

  In short: spline the control (Vind) because we need it continuously.
  Do not spline the states (Li, Ip, V) because learning them is the
  entire point.

WHY CUBIC SPLINE SPECIFICALLY? Cubic splines are C2-continuous —
  their first and second derivatives are smooth. This matters for the
  adjoint sensitivity method (InterpolatingAdjoint) we use to compute
  gradients during training. Noisy or discontinuous control signals
  produce noisy gradients, which destabilize training. The spline
  acts as a denoising and smoothing step as well as an interpolant.

ONE SPLINE PER SHOT: Each of the 489 shots has its own Vind time
  series and its own time axis. We precompute all 489 splines before
  training begins and store them in a dictionary keyed by shot ID.
  Inside the loss function, the appropriate spline is looked up for
  each shot. The spline evaluation is wrapped in Zygote.ignore() to
  prevent the automatic differentiation system from trying to
  differentiate through the spline's underlying C library calls,
  which it cannot do.

MULTIPLE SHOOTING — WHY AND HOW
------------------------------------------------------------------------
BACKGROUND: THE NAIVE APPROACH AND WHY IT FAILS

The simplest way to train a neural ODE is "single shooting": start
from the initial conditions at t=0, integrate forward to the end of
the shot, compare the predicted trajectory to the measured data, and
backpropagate the error. This works well on short, smooth trajectories
but fails badly on longer ones for two reasons.

First, small errors in the neural network accumulate over time. A
slightly wrong dV/dt at t=0.1s produces a slightly wrong V at t=0.2s,
which feeds back into dLi/dt and dIp/dt, which compounds the error
further. By t=0.4s the predicted trajectory may be wildly wrong, even
if the network is approximately correct everywhere. The loss landscape
seen by the optimizer becomes extremely nonlinear and hard to navigate.

Second, the adjoint gradient computation must integrate *backwards*
through the entire shot duration. Long integrations accumulate
floating point errors in both the forward and backward passes,
producing noisy or vanishing gradients. This is the "exploding/
vanishing gradient" problem in ODE form.

Wang et al. address both problems using multiple shooting, which is
standard practice in scientific machine learning for neural ODEs.

WHAT MULTIPLE SHOOTING DOES

Multiple shooting divides each shot's time axis into short overlapping
segments called groups. For a shot with 28 timesteps and a group size
of 5, you get roughly 6-7 segments. Each segment is solved as an
independent short ODE starting from fresh initial conditions at the
beginning of that segment.

  Segment 1: t[1]  → t[5],  starting from u[t=1]  (measured)
  Segment 2: t[5]  → t[9],  starting from u[t=5]  (measured)
  Segment 3: t[9]  → t[13], starting from u[t=9]  (measured)
  ...and so on.

Each segment is short enough that errors don't compound badly.
Gradients flow back only through the short segment, not the entire
shot, making them cleaner and more informative. The loss is computed
on each segment independently and summed.

THE CONTINUITY TERM

There is a problem with dividing the trajectory into independent
segments: the predicted state at the end of segment 1 may not match
the measured initial condition used to start segment 2. If you just
ignore this mismatch, the network may learn a solution that fits each
segment individually but produces a discontinuous overall trajectory
that is physically nonsensical.

The continuity term is a penalty added to the loss that penalizes
the gap between the predicted end state of one segment and the
measured start state of the next:

  continuity_loss = continuity_term × sum of |pred_end[i] - data_start[i+1]|

A continuity_term of 0 means segments are completely independent —
the network only needs to fit the data within each segment.
A continuity_term of 10 (as was tried early in this project) means
the continuity penalty dominates and the optimizer spends most of
its effort matching segment endpoints rather than fitting the physics.
We found continuity_term = 0 works best for this problem because
the measured data already provides the start of each segment.

GROUP SIZE

Group size controls how many timesteps are in each segment. It is
a tradeoff:

  Small group size (e.g. 3-4 timesteps):
    + Shorter integration, cleaner gradients
    + More segments, more loss terms per shot
    - Each segment contains very little trajectory information
    - Network can fit short segments with almost any weights

  Large group size (e.g. 10-15 timesteps):
    + Each segment contains more trajectory information
    + Stronger constraint on the learned dynamics
    - Longer integration, noisier gradients
    - Approaches single-shooting as group_size → len(shot)

Wang et al. use a group size of approximately 5 timesteps, which
balances gradient quality against trajectory information. In our
current multi-shot training loop we have moved away from the
DiffEqFlux multiple_shoot() function (which caused Zygote
compatibility issues with our per-shot splines) and instead solve
each shot as a single full-length integration. This is closer to
single shooting per shot, but because each shot is only 26-46
timesteps and we warm-start from measured initial conditions per
shot, the gradient quality is acceptable. If training stalls on
harder shots, reverting to explicit segment-wise multiple shooting
within each shot is the natural next step.

RELATION TO WANG'S IMPLEMENTATION

Wang implements multiple shooting in JAX using a custom scan-based
approach. The key architectural choice we share is that each shot
in the multi-shot training batch starts from its own measured initial
conditions — we do not chain shots together. This means the network
must learn dynamics that generalize across all initial conditions in
the training set, not just those in a single long trajectory. This
is precisely why training on 100 diverse shots (our current setting)
is far more informative than training on 1 shot for 100x as long.

THE HUBER LOSS FUNCTION — WHY IT MATTERS HERE
------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HUBER LOSS?

Most people default to mean squared error (MSE) as a loss function.
MSE penalizes errors as the square of their magnitude. This means a
prediction that is 10x wrong contributes 100x as much to the loss as
a prediction that is 1x wrong. Large errors dominate the gradient and
the optimizer fixates on fixing outliers at the expense of the bulk
of the data.

Huber loss is a hybrid. For small errors (below a threshold δ) it
behaves like MSE — quadratic, smooth, well-behaved gradients. For
large errors (above δ) it switches to linear penalty, like mean
absolute error (MAE). The result is a loss function that is:

  - Differentiable everywhere (unlike pure MAE, which has a
    non-differentiable kink at zero)
  - Robust to outliers (unlike MSE, which lets outliers dominate)
  - Sensitive to small errors (unlike MAE, which treats all errors
    equally regardless of sign or magnitude)

In formula form, for a scalar residual r and threshold δ:

  L(r) = 0.5 * r²/δ         if |r| ≤ δ   (quadratic region)
  L(r) = |r| - 0.5*δ        if |r| > δ   (linear region)

Our implementation uses relative errors (normalized by the true
value) before applying the Huber function, so the threshold δ=0.1
means "treat errors below 10% of the true value quadratically,
and errors above 10% linearly."

WHY IT SUITS THIS PROBLEM

The C-Mod dataset is experimental plasma data. It is noisy. Some
shots have measurement artifacts, aggressive transients near the
end of the discharge, or sudden events (like the -2.34 V spike at
the last timestep of shot 1050218005) that no smooth ODE model
should be expected to reproduce exactly. These are genuine outliers
in the training data.

With MSE loss, those outlier timesteps — particularly the violent
rampdown endpoints — would produce enormous gradients that swamp
the signal from the well-behaved portions of the trajectory. The
network would spend most of its capacity trying to fit the last
few timesteps of the hardest shots, at the expense of learning
the general physics that applies across all shots.

Huber loss caps the influence of those outlier timesteps. The
network learns from the full trajectory without being hijacked
by the hardest points.

WHY ALLEN LIKELY CHOSE IT — AND WHETHER IT WAS CRITICAL

Wang almost certainly chose Huber loss for the same reasons: he
was working with real experimental data from a fusion reactor,
which is inherently noisy and contains transient events. This is
a standard choice in any physics-ML paper dealing with experimental
time series rather than clean simulation data.

Was it critically beneficial? Probably yes for the MlpODE model,
less so for RomeroNNV. Here is the reasoning:

MlpODE asks a pure neural network to learn all three equations from
scratch. With no physics prior, the network is much more vulnerable
to being misled by outlier timesteps — it has no fallback of
"the physics says this should look like X." Huber loss's robustness
to outliers is therefore more important for MlpODE.

RomeroNNV only asks the network to learn a residual correction to
the third equation. The exact Romero equations for du[1] and du[2]
already constrain the solution strongly. Even with MSE loss,
RomeroNNV would likely be more stable than MlpODE because the
physics does most of the work. Huber loss is still beneficial —
it improves training stability and the quality of the learned
residual — but it is probably not the difference between success
and failure for RomeroNNV the way it may be for MlpODE.

This is actually a testable hypothesis within our project: if we
retrain MlpODE with MSE loss instead of Huber, we would expect
noticeably worse performance, while the same switch on RomeroNNV
would have a smaller effect.

OPTIMIZER CHOICE — AdamW WITH EXPONENTIAL LEARNING RATE DECAY
------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS AdamW?

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) is the de facto standard optimizer
for training neural networks. It maintains a running estimate of both
the first moment (mean gradient) and second moment (uncentered
variance of gradient) for each parameter. It uses these estimates to
adaptively scale the learning rate per parameter — parameters with
consistently large gradients get smaller effective learning rates,
and parameters with small or noisy gradients get larger ones. This
makes Adam much more robust than plain stochastic gradient descent
on problems where gradients vary widely in scale across parameters,
which is typical of neural ODEs where some parameters affect the
loss strongly and others barely at all.

AdamW is a corrected variant of Adam that fixes a subtle bug in how
Adam handles weight decay (L2 regularization). In the original Adam,
weight decay is folded into the gradient before the adaptive scaling,
which means the decay is applied unevenly across parameters. AdamW
decouples weight decay from the gradient update entirely, applying
it directly to the parameters after the gradient step:

  θ ← θ - η * adam_step(∇L) - λ * θ

where λ is the weight decay coefficient. This produces better
regularization behavior — it penalizes large weights uniformly
regardless of gradient magnitude.

Wang's configuration:
  optimizer = optax.adamw(learning_rate=schedule)
  where schedule = optax.exponential_decay(1e-3, 50, 0.9, end_value=2.5e-4)

Our Julia equivalent:
  OptimizationOptimisers.AdamW(0.001f0, (0.9f0, 0.999f0), 1f-4)

WHY WEIGHT DECAY FOR THIS PROBLEM?

Neural ODEs are prone to a specific failure mode: the network learns
large-magnitude weights that produce a correction term that perfectly
fits the training shots but generalizes poorly to held-out shots.
This is standard overfitting, but it is especially dangerous in ODEs
because large weights tend to produce stiff equations — the ODE
solver takes tiny timesteps to maintain accuracy, training slows
dramatically, and in extreme cases the ODE becomes numerically
unstable.

Weight decay keeps the network weights small, which has two benefits:
  1. It regularizes against overfitting across shots
  2. It keeps the NN correction term bounded, which keeps the ODE
     well-conditioned and numerically stable

This is directly related to why we also initialize the final layer
near zero. Both choices — near-zero initialization and weight decay
— are pushing in the same direction: start close to the Romero
baseline and only deviate from it when the data strongly demands it.

WHY AN EXPONENTIALLY DECAYING LEARNING RATE?

The exponential schedule Wang uses starts at η=1e-3 and decays by
a factor of 0.9 every 50 iterations, with a floor of η=2.5e-4:

  η(step) = max(1e-3 × 0.9^(step/50), 2.5e-4)

This reflects a well-understood pattern in neural network training:

Early training (high learning rate): The network is far from any
  good solution. Large steps are beneficial — they allow the
  optimizer to traverse the loss landscape quickly and escape flat
  regions or shallow local minima. Using a small learning rate
  early means taking many small steps in the wrong direction before
  finding the right basin.

Late training (low learning rate): The network is near a good
  solution. Large steps are harmful — they overshoot the minimum
  and cause the loss to oscillate rather than converge. A small
  learning rate allows fine-grained refinement of the parameters.

The decay rate of 0.9 every 50 steps is relatively slow — it takes
about 650 steps to halve the learning rate. This is intentional for
neural ODEs, where gradients are computed via adjoint integration
and are inherently noisier than standard backpropagation gradients.
A slower decay gives the optimizer more time at each learning rate
level to extract signal from noisy gradients before stepping down.

The floor of 2.5e-4 (one-quarter of the initial rate) prevents the
learning rate from decaying to near-zero before training is complete.
Without a floor, exponential decay eventually makes the optimizer
nearly stationary, which looks like convergence even if the model
has not actually converged.

WHY THIS MATTERS SPECIFICALLY FOR NEURAL ODEs

Standard neural network training computes gradients by backpropagating
through a fixed computational graph. The graph is the same every
iteration. Neural ODE gradients are computed by solving an adjoint
ODE backwards in time — the computational "graph" changes every
iteration because the forward ODE trajectory changes as the network
weights change. This means the gradient noise level is not constant
during training: early in training, when the network is far from a
good solution, the ODE trajectory is chaotic and adjoint gradients
are especially noisy. Later in training, when the trajectory is more
stable, gradients are cleaner.

The exponential decay schedule is well-matched to this dynamic.
The high early learning rate is robust to the noisy early gradients —
large steps are taken but the direction changes enough that the
optimizer still makes progress on average. The lower late learning
rate exploits the cleaner late-training gradients for precise
convergence. A fixed learning rate would require choosing between
"large enough to make early progress" and "small enough to converge
cleanly" — the schedule sidesteps this tradeoff entirely.

NOTE ON OUR CURRENT IMPLEMENTATION

Our current Julia implementation uses AdamW with a fixed learning
rate of 1e-3 rather than the full exponential decay schedule. The
decay schedule requires either a custom training loop or the
ParameterSchedulers.jl package, which adds complexity. The fixed
AdamW is a reasonable approximation — the weight decay provides
the regularization benefit, and the fixed rate produces acceptable
results. Implementing the full schedule is a natural next step if
training quality needs improvement.

TRAINING
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current configuration (v49):
  N_shots: 100 (first 100 shots by DataFrame order)
  Optimizer: AdamW(lr=0.001, β=(0.9,0.999), λ=1e-4)
  maxiters: 3000
  Loss: mean Huber loss on Li and Ip predictions across all shots
        (relative errors, δ=0.1)
  Sensealg: InterpolatingAdjoint(ZygoteVJP)

Key Zygote compatibility constraints:
  - All shot data (DataFrames) precomputed into plain Julia Dicts
    before the loss function — DataFrames not differentiable
  - Vind splines precomputed per shot outside the loss function
  - Spline evaluation wrapped in Zygote.ignore() inside ODE RHS
  - Near-zero initialization of final NN layer (scale factor 0.01)

Training loss plateau: ~0.175 after 3000 iterations
  - Believed to be near-irreducible for this shot set with current
    architecture — Romero already fits most gentle shots well
